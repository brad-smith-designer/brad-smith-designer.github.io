RBC - Multi Product Onboarding

A new way to open accounts at RBC.

My role at a glance

  • Found ways to increase client quality and new account aquisition, by allowing new clients to open multiple products at once
  • Co-led a small design team for a year to design, iterate on, and deliver a scaleable and realistic MVP
  • Worked with 6 separate design departments around RBC, in addition to product, legal, business, and development teams
  • Designed with data from 10+ research studies stemming from each design iteration
  • Balanced the needs of the business and our clients with regular presentations and stakeholder engagement

01What is MPO and why is it important to RBC?

I was briefed at the beginning of 2024 on Multi Product Onboarding (MPO). MPO started off as a solution for opening international accounts in response to our acquisition of HSBC Canada. But unexpectedly, what the business saw most value in wasnâ€™t just international accounts, it was the ability to open a whole package of accounts at once.

Since all our account open flows so far had been optimised around a single account, reinventing the experience around the idea of a package of accounts was an exciting unknown.

Pick up a PoC and run with it

I was asked to further explore the concept and deliver an MVP as lead interaction and visual designer, whilst partnering with a content designer who I worked alongside well into 2025.

  • Late 2022RBC announces purchase of HSBC Canada
  • Mid 2023Design team forms a PoC for International Banking Package to align with HSBC products
  • Late 2023Design and product teams expand scope to focus on Multi Product Onboarding
  • Early 2024Design (and myself) given go ahead to develop an MVP for launch in 2025

Increase client retention

Retention - the percentage of clients who stick around for at least 2 years - is only 50% for clients who have only one account. Adding a credit card boosts that number to 73%, and adding one or two engagement activities results in 85% and 99% respectively. Simply put, clients with more products that properly fit their daily needs are much more likely to stay longer.

Enhance the quality of new clients

The overall goal of MPO is to onboard new clients in a way which helps them settle in with RBC. Accounts, investments, loans, credit cards, even mortgages... Building that deep relationship from the start is the opportunity this hopes to unlock.

02Exploring what form MPO should take

There were some burning questions from the start, but I mainly wanted to know what clients would actually benefit from. Where is the sweet spot of 'upselling' that wouldn't alienate our single-product clients?

How many accounts do clients want?

According to 72% of multi-product interest clients, it's 2 or 3 accounts.

I decided to start off with the model of chequing account, savings account, and credit card, which describes the bank's 'ideal' client. We'd use this model going forward on which to base our design explorations, and eventually we'd add foreign currencies and US dollar to the mix.

How much guidance do clients want?

RBC offer 10 accounts within the chequing and savings categories, and the difference between them can be confusing. Some knowledgable clients know exactly which products they want, whilst others like to discuss their options with an advisor.

I produced 2 initial concepts at different ends of this scale, exploring the possibilities between passive and active product recommendations.

The early discovery work gave us some critical knowledge

These concepts were presented to would-be users to gather qualitative feedback via moderated tests. The outcome gave us a nuanced understanding of the 'sweet spot', which provided an early direction for the project.

  • Users like being recommended specific products, not just types of product. For example, the 'High Interest eSavings' instead of just 'a savings account'.
  • Users like to see all of their options, not just our recommendation. So alongside the best product, an easy way to see the other products within that category.
  • Users want to provide their actual banking needs to inform recommendations, instead of us using their age and client segment to make sweeping generalisations. A tag with the label 'popular' wouldn't cut it.

The next iteration would have to be more conversational

The main idea was to follow the same structure of conversation a client would have with an advisor. That meant:

  • Knowing their basic information like name, age, and whether they're a student or new to Canada
  • Listening to the client explain their specific banking needs
  • Allowing the client the opportunity to open more accounts, whilst seeing all the options, but respect their preference to stick to just one

Adapting our current account open experience

Our solution had to fit into the current user flow on the RBC public website. Over a few weeks a solution began to take shape, weaving in new screens and adjusting existing ones in a way that fit within our technical architecture.

I focussed on a flow which:

  • Learns as much as possible about the client upfront
  • Allows clients to choose between having a product-fit conversation, or 'skipping' with just one product
  • Presents product recommendations in context to their original selection, while offering 'skip' clients the same ability to freely explore and add products

03Getting buy-in from across the bank

As expected with a project as large as this, it would require the input of many teams, in charge of different digital channels, to produce a cohesive solution. Some of these teams would have to be sold the idea.

An experience map to show the problem

I produced a map for our entire current-state experience, across multiple flows and digital platforms, to show how convoluted the current experience could be for a multi-product client.

Then I produced a map for the future experience, to compare to current-state, and demonstrate the potential impact our work could have for our clients and their perception of RBC.

My approach involved:

  • Finding as much existing shared research as possible that's still relevant, plus data from GA, and applying it to the map.
  • Reaching out to researchers and designers across the bank to gather insights fron their research activities.
  • Using knowledge from our ongoing research MPO studies.
  • Roughly visualising each 'step' with user sentiments for easy shareouts.

The map worked as intended, and showed how much time we'd save a large number of our new clients.

Take it further

Soon I was asked by our director to extend this activity for another purpose - to kick off a week-long design sprint. For that I connected with design leads from other departments to get their direct input, and checked in periodically to provide guidance where needed.

Folding in our experience vision principles

Whilst mapping our journeys the team also agreed upon an experience vision, with basic principles that could be applied to our decision making. But this initiative was somewhat detached from the other design activities the team were doing.

I sought to fix that by starting with the journey map and pain points, bringing in the latest research findings, and aligning each finding with the relevant principle from that experience vision. This was put in a diagram that could be used to produce actionable user stories, which stay true to that experience vision.

Increasing visibility of the project

As the project went on throughout 2024 it became clear that MPO was an extremely popular subject for many teams within the bank.

My role became as much about protecting the original vision, as it was about engaging with stakeholders and socialising the initiative. Typically this involved some compromise, in part due to complex technical architecture, but also because the bank is subject to strict Canadian financial regulations.

The account open flow would need re-engineered

The 'sales rail', the flow of screens by which we collect client information, is a linear and rigid series of forms within a single page app (SPA). They were completely different between channels, so I helped start the conversations with our tech and product partners which would be required to embark on a technical overhaul.

Much of the early work involved creating diagrams whilst having conversations with other design partners. With a diagram, I could map out our ideal account open flow and communicate how the technical implementation of our existing flow would have to change to accommodate it.

I created this particular diagram to communicate the high-level effort required for each release. Our product and tech partners found this useful for writing stories and knowing where to focus their attention next.

Planning our updates within a busy team

One of the key takeaways was orchestrating our planned user flow updates amongst a flurry of releases from roughly 10 squads.

Sharing the work at refinement sessions, and adapting our plan where necessary, were both crucial to make sure the journey's releases formed a cohesive whole.

04The nitty gritty

Catering to two types of client

The point where we offer the client the opportunity to discuss banking needs for other products, was under a lot of scrutiny. On the one hand, we should encourage clients to share some financial needs to achieve maximum efficiency of funds. On the other hand, some clients are simply not interested in that, and those people are just as important.

This 'banking needs' screen went through countless iterations, framing our question in different ways to draw in multi-product clients without upsetting single-product clients.

Creating rudimentary UI components to work with

MPO had user needs that no other RBC product had, therefore a new set of UI components would be made to support them.

I used RBC's design system to create this UI, which provided a basis for our prototyping and iteration. Fonts, text sizes, text weights, colours, buttons, and pills were provided to maintain consistency with other RBC experiences.

Whilst consistent, it can't be ignored that RBC's design language lends the UI a utilitarian feel.

Off-canvas product exploration

Users expressed concern that if they chose to explore and add other products, it would take them away from their spot in the flow

I chose to adapt an existing UI component to house menus for categories, individual products, and detail views for each product. This pattern would also afford us the luxury of adding more guidance content when required, such as offers and product nuances that would come up in conversation with an advisor.

The product comparison card

We asked users what features they prioritised when making a decision to move forwad with an account.

For a chequing account, 69.4% of users said monthly fee was most important aspect to compare. But for a savings account, 61.1% said that interest rate was deemed the most important.

Interestingly, promotions were considered the least important aspect. However, two things led to the design below: consistency with how promotions are treated on our marketing site, and a strong interest from our product team who wanted to highlight short-term offers more effectively. Despite going against the grain of the data, the selection card tested very well in subsequent tests.

The multi-product selector

The solution I developed included a screen called the multi-product selector. It consists of a blue section dedicated to exploring and adding other products, and a white section focussed on their selections in the fashion of a cart. The user would get to this screen, either having answered some questions about their banking needs, or skipping that with just one product.

For users who skipped, the screen offers a chance to browse and add anything else whilst reviewing their selections. This was designed to be as gentle a sell as possible for clients who were in a hurry.

For users who answered questions about their banking needs, the blue section would adapt to show their recommended products with a series of product cards.

Making sure clients finish the flow with confidence

Since this onboarding is potentially more complex than usual, I wanted to devote some effort to improving the experience at the end.

I redesigned the screens for reviewing your new accounts and being informed of next steps. Key considerations included:

  • Clarity around which accounts are opened and with which bank
  • Guidance to take note of the client number, to log into Online Banking, since it wouldn't be supplied again for 7-10 days
  • Continuity between the onboarding flow and any subsequent communication, like emails
  • Reassurance that any offers or promotions are still there, and how to get them

Showing new accounts across two banks

The old version of this screen was designed around just one account, at just one bank.

The layout I used here is meant to visually represent the group of accounts which sit under each bank, RBC Royal Bank Canada and RBC Bank U.S., while giving the user guidance about which piece of information they need to save in order to start banking immediately.

Clear next steps

This particular example shows instructions to copy the client number and use it to access the RBC Mobile app, followed by a number and link to access the separate U.S. Online Banking portal.

Care was taken to ensure that the messaging at the end of the flow was echoed in the email.

05Connecting teams to make things simple

Once our users had become clients and opened their accounts, next came the tricky task of getting them onboarded to two separate banks' online banking platforms.

Mixing Canadian and U.S. accounts involves two separate banks - RBC Royal Bank of Canada and RBC Bank U.S. Design leadership were keen to push a 'north-south' approach, meaning that the Canadian side would always be the primary touchpoint, with the U.S. side secondary to that.

Guiding our new client during the onboarding phase

I realised early-on that simply explaining the reason for the different platforms would confuse anyone, not least our weary clients who were by now probably tired of filling out forms. So providing an easy-to-follow singular path through all the complexity was my goal.

Our client would have just finished the account-open flow on their web browser. An existing feature outside my control was that, at the end and depending on their device, they'd be directed to Online Banking on desktop or the RBC Mobile app on mobile.

Their journey would probably look like:

  • Copy their client number in the web browser, at the end of the flow
  • Follow a primary link to either the Online Banking portal in the browser, or RBC Mobile app on the app store
  • Log into these for the first time using their recently-copied client number
  • Access and familiarise with their Canadian accounts and profile
  • Follow links to enrol in the U.S. Online Banking, which can only happen in the browser
  • Access and familiarise with their U.S. accounts and profile
  • Follow links to connect both platorms and allow cross-border money transfers

Finding the right components with other teams

Teams including Online Banking, Mobile, and the U.S. design team and vendors, were consulted and tasked with building various new communications and entry points within their platforms. These included their designers, tech leads and architects from respective journeys.

I did an initial exploration of the tools available within those platforms, made suggestions on which to use, then held some discussions with those teams to collect their input and gain alignment. These solutions were brought to refinements where I explained the need and our approach to stakeholders.

Contextual communication

In additional to providing content in the right places, I wanted to provide the right content for clients at different stages of their enrolment.

U.S. enrolment for clients just starting off. Account linking for clients already enrolled to U.S. Account visibility for clients enrolled and linked.

I identified these stages, communicated the need for contextual content to our stakeholders, and worked with product and tech to allow us to show content based on different states of enrolment. This required tech alignment between Canadian and U.S. teams which had previously been considred too ambiguous to attempt.

More prototypes please!

I encouraged the use of variables within Figma, providing some coaching to other designers, to enhance our prototyping capabilities.

Because of this I became the go-to person on our team to help others with their prototyping.

06Designing an alternative approach

During the project it became clear that our solution was becoming more and more linear, with users given different experiences based on past choices.

I suspected this could be a restrictive architecture and less scaleable than we'd like.

Whilst whiteboarding for a separate issue, I drew up another, different, clean-slate solution for selecting multiple products and getting advice.

Browse first, help second

While the original idea was to ask the client questions about their banking needs prior to exploring account options, this idea would allow the user to explore first, then get help choosing their account only if they need it.

I prioritised simplicity and removed as much complexity from the screen as possible.

The team ran an A/B test using my concept and another, during which this one performed better in terms of user sentiment.

This idea, whilst on the sidelines and relatively untested, kept cropping up in the following months' discussions. It found favour amongst the team as a fresh solution to a rapidly growing design problem.
